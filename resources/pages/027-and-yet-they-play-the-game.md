And then this — which is the entire ethic of the poem in five words.

You see almost nothing. You misunderstand most of what you do see. You take your sad creations for reality. You speak to almost no one. You build things you cannot perceive in a reality you cannot access. The cosmic vantage looking at the human condition could despair.

*And yet they play the game.*

You play. You wake up and try. You show up for the day even when you don't feel like it. You return to the work that has not yet rewarded you. You stay with the people in your life through the ordinary frictions of two lives sharing a kitchen, a friendship, a family. You return to the thing you've been making for years even though no one has seen it yet. You play. You did not give up.

This is the universe's verdict on the human condition, delivered with something like awe. *Look at them. Look at what they don't have. Look at what they do anyway.* The "and yet" carries the entire weight. It is the same "and yet" that runs through every act of grace ever recorded — *and yet she got up. And yet he forgave him. And yet they kept building. And yet, and yet, and yet.*

The universe loves you for the *and yet*. Not for being wise, not for being awakened, not for having figured anything out. For continuing to play the game in a fog of partial information with a sad creation in your head and a black sun overhead some days. That is the heroism the cosmos sees in you.
