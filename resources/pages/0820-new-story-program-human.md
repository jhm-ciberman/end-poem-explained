And then the triple definition. Three sentences, three parallel structures, three different vocabularies for what you are.

*A new story, never told before.* You are a narrative. The genome that composes you is, the voice claims, *letters* — a written record. The four nucleotide bases are an alphabet. The arrangement of them in your genome is a sentence that has never been written before, because the particular shuffling of your father's and mother's chromosomes that produced your unique combination is statistically unprecedented and will never be repeated. *Never told before.* You are a story the universe has not heard until now.

*A new program, never run before.* You are software. The same DNA, viewed in a different vocabulary, is *code*. It is executable. When the cells read it and act on it, they are *running* the program. And the program of *you* — your specific build — has never been executed before. Even if every bit of your code came from somewhere — your eyes from one ancestor, your laugh from another — the *integration* of those bits, the runtime of the whole stack, is fresh. The program is being run for the first time, in real time, right now.

The program metaphor lands hard if you stop and consider what you are. Code is a set of instructions that, when run, produces a living process. That is exactly what your body is doing in this second — running the instructions written into your DNA, producing the living process that is *you*. You are the running program. The execution. The active state. And the *sourcecode* that generated you is *a billion years old* — every line was inherited from the long evolutionary chain, debugged across millions of generations, refactored by every survival event that ever happened to your ancestors. The instructions are ancient. The instance is new. You are the most recent run of the oldest program in the world.

*A new human, never alive before, made from nothing but milk and love.* And then the third definition, which is the one that will catch you in the throat. After the high-tech vocabulary of *story* and *program*, the voice arrives at the simplest possible description. *A new human.* And the materials list: *milk and love.*

*Milk* — literal. You were fed by a body. The first food you had came from another person's body, made specifically for you. The milk was a complete account of how to grow into a small functioning human, transmitted through a substance.

*Love* — also literal, though the voice is using a vocabulary that sounds metaphorical. The voice is saying that love was an actual material in your construction. The attention paid to you, the holding, the looking, the response when you cried, the staying — these are not optional decorative features of human development. Without them you would not have become a working human. Infants without sustained loving attention fail to thrive. The studies are unambiguous. *Love* is not a sentimental ornament. It is structural.

So the voice's materials list — *nothing but milk and love* — is honest. The minimum required inputs to produce a working person are: nutrition and sustained loving attention. Everything else is decoration. You were built out of those two things. The voice is naming them as the actual ingredients.

The phrase *nothing but* is doing work too. *Nothing but milk and love* — meaning, that's all it took. The grandeur of you, the entire complexity of who you have become, traces back to those two simple inputs administered patiently over years. The cosmos required nothing more elaborate than a lactating body and a loving one to make a player.
