And then, the gathering.

*A woman gathered the atoms.* Your mother. The voice does not name her, but you know who is meant. The woman who was the chosen instrument of your assembly. She walked through her life — drinking water, eating bread and meat and fruit, breathing air — and through these ordinary acts she was *unknowingly collecting the matter of her future child*. Every meal she ate during the months that mattered was, in retrospect, a contribution to your construction. The grass became a cow became a steak became a meal became a molecule became a cell of you. The river became drinking water became blood became a cell of you. The air became oxygen became a cell of you.

Your mother did not know, in the moments she was eating and drinking and breathing, that she was assembling you. She just lived. The assembly happened anyway. The voice frames this with extraordinary tenderness: *she drank and ate and inhaled; and the woman assembled the player, in her body.*

*Assembled.* This is a beautiful word for what gestation actually is. A human body is not grown from scratch. It is *assembled* from materials gathered from the surrounding world by another body that gathers and processes and routes and constructs. You were a construction project. The construction site was your mother. The materials were the local biosphere. The blueprint was the DNA. And the result was you.

Anyone who has ever cooked a meal — gathering ingredients, processing them, combining them into something whole — has done a small version of what your mother did. The voice is using the vocabulary of *assembly* — building from parts — for what happened over nine months in her body. She *assembled* you out of what she could obtain from the environment, with no manual, no warning, and no way to step back and look at the work.
