---
lines:
  - voice: green
    text: "Does it know that we love it? That the universe is kind?"
---

The voice asks the question with something like anxiety. *Does it know?* As if it might not. As if the love is being given but not received.

This is one of the most vulnerable lines in the poem because of what it admits about the cosmic perspective. The universe is not indifferent here. The universe is not cold mechanism, or impersonal law, or a watchmaker who wound the spring and walked away. The universe is *worried* that you might not feel loved. It cares about your reception of its care. It wants to be known to be kind.

There's a theological move happening that's worth naming. Most cosmologies make you choose: either the universe is personal and loving (the religious frame), or it is vast and indifferent (the scientific frame). Gough refuses the choice. The universe in this poem is *both* — physically enormous, made of stars and quarks and statistical mechanics, *and* affectionate, attentive, bent toward you specifically. The love is not separate from the physics. The love is what the physics is *for*.

And the question is asked in plural — "does it know that *we* love it?" The voices speak collectively. Whatever they are, they are not singular. The love being offered is choral, not from a single throne.
