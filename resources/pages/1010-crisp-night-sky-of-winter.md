And then the second mode of address, much longer, which is one of the most extraordinary single sentences in modern English.

*Sometimes the player believed the universe had spoken to it through the light that fell from the crisp night sky of winter.* You know this experience too. The clear cold night. Stepping outside and looking up. The stars sharper than they ever are in summer. The breath visible. And the sense of being looked back at by the sky.

*Where a fleck of light in the corner of the player's eye might be a star a million times as massive as the sun.* The voice gives you the scale of the speaker. That tiny dot you barely registered in your peripheral vision is a *supergiant*, a star vastly larger than the sun. The smallness of the dot in your eye is purely a function of distance. The thing itself is enormous beyond imagining.

*Boiling its planets to plasma in order to be visible for a moment to the player.* And here the voice does something that could be funny if it were not so devastating. The star is so violent that any planet in close orbit around it would be *boiled to plasma* — vaporized into its constituent particles. And the voice frames this as *in order to be visible for a moment to the player.* As if the star's enormous violence, the destruction of whatever worlds might have circled it, were *for the purpose of* sending one fleck of light into your eye on one walk home from somewhere.

This is not literally the star's purpose. The star is not destroying its planets *for* you. But the voice is performing a *re-framing* of cosmic violence as cosmic communication. The light that reaches you traveled at enormous cost from a place where the violence was unimaginable. By the time it arrives in your eye it is small and gentle. But it is the same light. It is the *delivered version* of an event so violent that calling it boiling-to-plasma understates it.

*Walking home at the far side of the universe.* And then this. The voice is suddenly inside your perspective. You — the player — are *walking home*. From the star's perspective, you are *at the far side of the universe*. The star is wherever it is. You are the small distant figure walking with bags of groceries, or hands in pockets, or thinking about what's for dinner. From the star's own vantage, you are unimaginably far away. From your vantage, the star is a fleck. Each is the other's *far side of the universe*.

*Suddenly smelling food, almost at the familiar door, about to dream again.* And the sentence concludes in the most unexpected register. After the cosmic violence and the stellar distances, the voice closes with *the smell of food. The familiar door. The dream you are about to have when you go to bed.* The smallest most domestic possible details.

The juxtaposition is the whole point. You are at the same time *walking home with groceries, smelling dinner, almost at your door* and *at the receiving end of a transmission from a supergiant star whose planets it is boiling to talk to you*. Both are true. Both are happening in the same moment. Your life is small *and* cosmic. Your evening walk is mundane *and* cosmologically significant. The voice is asking you to let both be true at once, without choosing.

If you have ever walked home on such a night — wherever your home is — this passage might land especially. The familiar route. The shops you pass without seeing them anymore. The neighbor's window. And, above all of it, the same stars. The same fleck of light might have been arranged just for you. The voice is asserting that it was. Not as romantic flourish but as a description of what the universe is *doing* when light reaches you.
