The scale extends in a different dimension. Not just *how much mass* but *how much distance*. And the voice measures the distance in the most appropriate possible unit: *the time it takes for light to cross it.*

Light, the fastest thing the physics permits, three hundred million meters per second, takes *eight minutes* to get from the Sun to your skin. Which means: the sunlight you feel is eight minutes old. The sun you see in the sky is the sun as it was eight minutes ago. The sun *now*, this instant, will not reach you for another eight minutes. You are looking, always, into the past of the sun.

This is one of the small wonders that physics keeps quietly handing to anyone who pays attention. *Nothing you see is now.* The light from your laptop screen is nanoseconds old. The light from across the room is microseconds. The light from the sun is eight minutes. The light from the nearest star is four years. The light from the most distant galaxies is thirteen billion years. You do not see the universe; you see *the universe's history*, ranked by distance. Every act of vision is also an act of time travel.

And the voice presents this casually. As if it were just a fact about your home. As if you should already know, and probably had forgotten, that you live in a place where the light is eight minutes late.
