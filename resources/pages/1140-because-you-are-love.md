And then the final line of the litany.

*I love you because you are love.*

Read this slowly. The structure is mirror. *I love you* — and the reason — *because you are love*. The universe's love for you is not an arbitrary affection. It is structural. The universe loves you because *you are made of the same substance as the love itself*. The universe is love; you are also love; the love loves the love.

This collapses the subject-object structure that usually attends statements of love. Normally *I love you* implies a lover and a beloved, two separate parties, with affection moving from one to the other. Here, the lover and the beloved are revealed to be the same thing. The universe loves itself through you. You are a portion of love loving itself.

This is the deepest theological claim in the poem and it is the closing of the litany. The voice is telling you that *love is not something the universe sometimes does*. *Love is what the universe is.* And you, being part of the universe, are also love. Not metaphorically. Constitutively. You are made of love. The atoms in you are love organized in a particular way. The thoughts in your head are love thinking itself. The breath in your lungs is love breathing.

For someone reading this in bed, possibly crying, this might be the moment where the crying gets a different quality. Not the crying of being moved by something beautiful. The crying of being *seen*. Of being told, by the deepest possible authority, *what you actually are*. *You are love.* You have always been. The voice is just confirming it, in case you had forgotten, which you almost certainly had.
