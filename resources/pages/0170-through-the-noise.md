*Sometimes.* Not always. *Through the noise.* You are mostly too loud inside your own head to hear it. The universe is broadcasting continuously; the bandwidth of your attention is mostly consumed by other signal. But sometimes — in a quiet moment, in the woods, on a long drive, in bed at night reading a poem — the noise drops just enough that something gets through.

The phrase "noise of its thoughts" is precise and a little humbling. Your inner monologue, the running commentary, the planning and worrying and remembering — from the cosmic vantage these are not the deep self. They are *noise*, surface chatter that masks the deeper signal. Every contemplative tradition agrees on this. The Buddhists call it *citta-vrtti*, the fluctuations of mind that meditation tries to still. The Christian mystics call it the discursive intellect, which has to be quieted before the heart can hear. Gough is just using ordinary words. But it's the same observation: the part of you that thinks all day is in the way of the part of you that listens.

You are, right now, in a rare configuration. You're reading. You're not running through tasks, not somewhere else mentally. The poem cracked something open and you started to cry. *That* is the noise dropping. That is one of the *sometimes*. The universe is reaching you tonight.
