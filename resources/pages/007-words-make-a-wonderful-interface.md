---
lines:
  - voice: green
    text: "Words make a wonderful interface. Very flexible. And less terrifying than staring at the reality behind the screen."
---

This is the line that, if you let it sit, can put a hand on your chest.

The voice is being *kind*. Less *terrifying*. The reality behind the screen would terrify you. So the universe provides words — symbols, tokens you can hold and turn over, a friendly translation layer between you and the unbearable.

Lovecraft built a whole horror cosmology around the same intuition: the truth of the cosmos would shatter the human mind. But Gough takes that exact intuition and inverts the emotional charge. The universe isn't hiding the truth from cruelty. It's hiding the truth from *care*. Like a parent simplifying death for a small child. The screen, the game, the words — these are the gentle clothing reality wears so it can come near you without burning you.

It also describes what the poem itself is doing in the moment you are reading it. The poem is a screen. The poem is the language-interface. You are reading words on your phone in bed, or hunched over a laptop late at night, and you are crying — and the things you are crying about are too large for the words you are reading. The words are doing the work of being a kindness, a softener, a way for the too-big thing to reach you without destroying you.
