And here is the line that, depending on where you are, can either comfort or wound — and sometimes both.

The universe could, in principle, lift your sadness. The voices imply they have the capacity. But they will not. *To cure it of sorrow would destroy it.* Your sorrow is doing something. It is part of who you are becoming. Removing it would not heal you; it would erase the version of you that the sorrow is helping to make.

This is hard. It is the hardest doctrine in any wisdom tradition that takes suffering seriously. The Buddhist tradition does not promise the cessation of all difficulty; it promises a transformed relationship with it. The Christian tradition does not promise that the cup will be taken away; Gethsemane is the model. The Stoics, the Sufis, the Jewish tradition of *yissurin shel ahavah* — sufferings of love — all converge on the same uncomfortable truth: the sorrow is not the enemy. The sorrow is part of the becoming.

"Its own private task." The phrase is almost startling in its respect. Your sorrow is *yours*. It belongs to you. It has work to do that no one else can do for you and no one else can witness. The voices respect the boundary. They will not reach across it.

"We cannot interfere." Not *we will not* — *we cannot*. There is some structural feature of how the dream works that prevents the rescue. To live a real life is to face certain things alone, even if the universe is watching with love.

If you have ever wondered why the prayers don't always get answered, why the suffering is allowed, why a loving cosmos doesn't just step in — the poem's answer is here. Not because the love is fake. Not because no one is listening. Because the rescue would undo the work.
