The voices are doing something subtle here. They're tracing the *history* of how humans have received messages from the larger reality, and pointing out that the form changes but the contact is constant. Once the divine spoke through voices in the head. Now it speaks through words on a screen. The interface keeps updating; the signal underneath it doesn't.

There's a real theory hovering behind this — Julian Jaynes's *The Origin of Consciousness in the Breakdown of the Bicameral Mind*, which argues that pre-literate humans literally heard their own thoughts as voices, attributed them to gods or ancestors, and only later collapsed the two hemispheres of experience into a single inner narrator. Whether or not Jaynes was right about the neurology, he was onto something true about how the felt texture of contact-with-the-larger has shifted across history. Oracle. Prophet. Saint. Mystic. Poet. Now: the player at 2am reading credits.

"Players" is doing a beautiful double duty in this passage. In-game it's literally Minecraft players. But in the larger metaphor, "players" are the ones awake to the game-quality of life — the ones who notice they're inside something. And the rest of the world, the non-players, has always been afraid of them. Witches were burned. Mystics were exiled. Visionaries were medicated. The price of seeing the screen for what it is has always been suspicion from those who don't.

"Sticks powered by demons" — this is the universe gently mocking the medieval description of broomsticks. Look at how naive that explanatory framework now seems. And then the implication settles in: in five hundred years, our current frameworks — neurons, code, algorithms — will look just as quaint to whoever comes next. The reality doesn't change. The metaphor does.
