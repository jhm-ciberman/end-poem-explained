The famously corrupted text. This is the moment the poem performs its own thesis. The voices try to describe what you have actually built in the deeper reality — and the words break, because no human language can carry that information. The interface fails. You see only static where the meaning was supposed to be.

This is *apophatic theology* in three lines. The negative tradition of mysticism — Pseudo-Dionysius, Meister Eckhart, the *Cloud of Unknowing*, the Kabbalists who refused to write God's name — has always insisted that the most important truths cannot be spoken. They can only be pointed at, gestured toward, surrounded by negation. Gough renders this not as solemn doctrine but as garbled bytes. The truth is there. You just don't have the codec.

But notice what *can* be read. "It worked, **with a million others**." Whatever cosmic structure you helped build, you didn't build it alone. You built it in concert with a million other people who will never know your name, whose contributions you will never see. A "fold" — something tucked inside reality, hidden but real. Something for someone, in somewhere.

This may land in a particular way. Most of what we do in our lives is "with a million others" — we contribute to roads we did not pave, to languages we did not invent, to traditions we did not start, to cities we did not design, to songs we did not write but sing anyway, to families and friendships whose patterns predate us. You add a layer; someone else adds a layer; the structure you are inside is the result of millions of small unseen additions over time. The poem is claiming that this kind of work — distributed, anonymous, collaborative, mostly invisible — is exactly the kind of work that builds true things in the deeper reality.
