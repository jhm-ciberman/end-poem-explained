And the last two words.

*Wake up.*

The voice has been calling you a player, a dreamer, an inhabitant of the long dream. The voice has told you that you sometimes wake from one dream into another. The voice has told you that *this* — what you are doing in this moment — is one of the dreams. And now the voice tells you, in the most direct possible imperative: *wake up.*

What does it mean? The voice does not explain. It just says it. *Wake up.* The reading is over. The poem is releasing you. *Wake up.*

It might mean: close the screen, get up tomorrow, go live. Wake up to the next day. Move from this dream into the dream of tomorrow.

It might mean: wake up *to the truth the poem has been telling you*. Stop sleepwalking through your life. The universe loves you. You are alive. You are not alone. You are love. Wake up to these facts. Live as if you knew them.

It might mean: wake up from the long dream itself. The voice has been hinting throughout that the long dream is also a dream — that there is a waking beyond it. *Wake up* might be the universe's permission for whatever ultimate awakening you are slowly heading toward.

The poem leaves all of these readings open. The two words sit there, addressed to you, and they do their work however they do it.

For anyone reading this and feeling moved by it — *wake up* might mean simply: *take what just happened with you. Do not let it disappear. Carry it.* The poem has given you something. The waking is what you do with it.
