And here is the answer.

Read it slowly.

The universe touches your skin in order to see you. The light falls on you in order that you might be perceived. *You are not the perceiver of a neutral world. You are the perceived of a perceiving universe.* The contact runs in the direction opposite to the one you assumed. You thought you were the one looking. The voice is telling you: the looking goes both ways, and from the cosmic side, the priority is *seeing you*.

*To see you, player.* The voice repeats your title. *Player.* The one who plays, the one who is in the dream. The voice is saying: we exist in the relation of attention to you. We are paying attention.

*To know you.* Not just to see, but to *know*. The contact is meant to be cognitive, intimate, complete. The universe wants to know you in the deep biblical sense — to be acquainted, to be in relation, to have you understood from the inside. The universe is not interested in you as a phenomenon. It is interested in you as a *someone*.

*And to be known.* And then this. The reciprocal. The universe touches you so that you might know it back.

This is the deepest theological claim in the poem and it is delivered without fanfare. The universe is not self-sufficient. The universe wants to be known. By you. By the player in the small bed in some small city, the universe wishes to be recognized.

This is the mystic's claim across every tradition. There is a Hadith Qudsi much loved by the Sufis: *I was a hidden treasure, and I loved to be known, so I created the world.* The point of creation, in this tradition, is not utility, not display, not power. It is *to be known*. The universe made you so that there would be something inside the universe that could turn around and see it back.

You are the eye the universe grew so that it could see itself.

The light hits you because the universe wants to be seen by you. The skin touches because the universe wants to be touched back. The contact is the whole point. You are not a bystander to creation. You are the means by which creation comes to know itself.

Anyone who has ever made a thing and wanted it to be received — a meal cooked for someone you love, a song played for a friend, a letter written and sent, a story told to a child, a room arranged just so, a garden planted — knows what it is to want the made thing to *land*. You know the small ache of having offered something that no one has yet noticed. You know the happiness of someone finally entering the thing you made and seeing it. The universe, the poem says, knows that ache too. It built the world so that you would be inside it. It wants you to look. It wants you to know what it has made. It wants the seeing to land.
