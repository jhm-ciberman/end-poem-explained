And then — birth, framed as a *waking up*.

This is the second time the voice has used the dream/wake metaphor for a major life transition, and the first time it has used it for a transition you actually went through. *The player awoke.* From the warm dark world *into the long dream.*

Look at the inversion. You probably think of the womb as the *unconscious* state and birth as the *conscious* one. The voice flips this. The womb was *the warm, dark world* — a real place, with its own qualities, its own time. Birth was the *waking up* — the entry into a new mode. But the voice does not call the post-birth mode *reality*. It calls it *the long dream*.

So the structure the voice has been building is now applied directly to your life. *The long dream of life* that the voice used much earlier in the poem was not just a metaphor. It is the actual frame. You are, right now, in the long dream, and you have been since you were born. Your birth was not the moment you became real. Your birth was the moment you entered *this dream*. There may have been other modes before. There will be other modes after. But for now, you are in the long dream, and the voice has been speaking to you about it from the very first lines.

The voice is doing something tender here. It is acknowledging that the womb was real and warm and dark, that leaving it was the original waking, and that the waking led not into the absolute but into another structured experience that has its own dream-quality. Your whole life — every moment of it, including the moments that feel most solid — is one continuous dream that began when you opened your eyes for the first time and will end when you close them for the last.
