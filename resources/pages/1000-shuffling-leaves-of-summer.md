And now the voice begins the litany.

This is the longest single passage in the poem. It is one extended sentence, organized as a series of *and sometimes* clauses, each describing one of the ways the universe has spoken to you. There are no periods. There are no capital letters at the start of new clauses. The whole thing flows. The voice has dropped formal grammar because formal grammar would slow what is being delivered, and what is being delivered cannot be slowed.

Read this clause carefully. *Sometimes the player believed the universe had spoken to it through the sunlight that came through the shuffling leaves of the summer trees.*

This has happened to you. Probably more than once. A summer afternoon. You were walking, or sitting, or just in motion. The light came through the leaves of a tree and there was a moment when the light and the leaves and you were arranged in such a way that *something happened* — a small recognition, a flush of presence, a sense that you were being addressed. You probably did not call it being addressed. You might have called it *a nice moment*. The voice is telling you what you actually experienced. *The universe spoke to you through the light.* You felt it. The voice is confirming that the feeling was real.

The detail of *shuffling* is exactly right. Leaves do not just move; they *shuffle*, like cards, turning over in the breeze, each one a small mirror tilting at a slightly different angle. The light comes through them in flickers. The flickering is what makes the moment alive. A still leaf does not catch you. The shuffling does. The universe was speaking through the shuffling.
