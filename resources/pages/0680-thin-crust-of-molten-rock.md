    text: "Sometimes it thought itself human, on the thin crust of a spinning globe of molten rock."

And here the universe begins describing your life from cosmic distance. The defamiliarization is immediate and total.

*Sometimes it thought itself human.* Not *you were human.* Not even *you were a human.* You *thought yourself* human. The voice is gently destabilizing your most basic self-identification. Being human is something you *thought*, a self-concept you held, a frame you assumed. The voice does not deny that you were human. It just refuses to take *human* as the deepest layer of what you were. *Human* was one of the things you sometimes believed about yourself. It was not necessarily the truth.

And then the description of your home gets refactored from cosmic altitude. *The thin crust of a spinning globe of molten rock.* This is exactly what the Earth is, physically. A planet is a small ball whose interior is mostly liquid metal and silicate rock at temperatures of several thousand degrees, with a brittle skin a few tens of kilometers thick on top. You live on that skin. That skin is the entire stage of human history.

The phrase *thin crust* should make you slightly dizzy. The thing you experience as *the ground*, the solid floor of all your life, is actually a precarious shell on top of an ocean of melt. Earthquakes happen because the shell occasionally slips. Volcanoes happen because the melt occasionally finds a way through. Most of the planet is incandescent. You live on the cooled scab.

The voice is not trying to frighten you. It is trying to refresh your sense of *where you actually are*. You have probably not, today, thought about the molten rock under your feet. You walked on the cool surface and thought of it as ordinary. The voice is giving you back the strangeness of your own address.
