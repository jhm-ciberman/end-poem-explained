And now the most beautifully chosen word in the entire poem. *Respawn.*

You know what *respawn* means. You die in the game and you come back at the spawn point, ready to play again. The poem takes that exact game-mechanic word and uses it for what is happening now: *you are coming back into the long dream, ready to play again.*

The credits are ending. The game is over. The universe, instead of saying *return to your life* or *go back to reality*, says *respawn*. It honors the game-frame even as it hands you back to the larger frame. It says: the long dream of life is *also* a game in which you respawn. You died — went into the cosmic conversation, lost yourself in the dialogue, became disembodied — and now you are being given a new spawn, a fresh entry, a chance to play the next round.

The word also gently undoes some of the fear of death. The universe is treating death as a respawn point. Without insisting on any particular doctrine — without saying *and you will go to heaven* or *and you will be reincarnated* — it uses a vocabulary in which coming back is normal. You die in the game; you respawn. The implication is left open, but the affective tone is set. Death, in the universe of this poem, is a checkpoint, not a wall.
