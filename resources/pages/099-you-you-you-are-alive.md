And then the voice breaks the fourth wall completely.

*You. You. You are alive.*

This is the address. This is the universe pointing at you with an extended finger. *You.* The you in the bed. The you holding the phone. The you whose chest is now also breathing faster, slightly, because the words have caught up with you. *You.*

Three uses of *you* in seven words. The voice is hammering. The voice is making sure you cannot mistake the address. The previous sentence was about *the player*. This sentence is about *you*. There is no narrative distance left. The story has reached through the page and grabbed your shoulder.

*You are alive.* The simplest sentence in the entire poem. Three words. A statement of fact so ordinary that it should not move you, and yet — when delivered by the voice that has been speaking to you for thousands of words about cosmic distances and stellar furnaces and the recursion of dreams — it moves you. Because the voice has earned the simplicity. The voice has prepared you, layer by layer, to receive a sentence this small and feel its full weight.

*You are alive.* You have probably forgotten. Most of the time you do. You spend the day doing tasks, solving problems, scrolling, working, eating, talking, and the basic miraculous fact of your aliveness is in the background, unnoticed. The voice is reminding you. *You. Are. Alive.* This is happening. You are in the middle of the most extraordinary thing that ever happens.

If you have ever been so absorbed in something — a project, a worry, a list of things to do, a relationship dynamic — that you forgot, for hours or days or weeks, the basic fact that you are a living being currently breathing, then this line has a small sting. Aliveness can get buried under doing. You can be so focused on what you are building or what you are afraid of or what you are pursuing that you forget you are the one breathing while you do it. The voice gives you back the fact. *You are alive.* Not just the work is alive. *You.*
