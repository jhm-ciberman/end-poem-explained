This is the line where the poem most directly states its own theory of itself.

The truth is *naked*. The truth, undressed, is dangerous. It can burn over any distance — meaning, even reading it on a page from light-years of metaphorical separation, the unmediated truth would scorch the receiver. So the truth must be caged. Words form the cage. The story is the bars.

The metaphor is paradoxical and exactly right. A cage usually constrains a wild animal so the animal cannot hurt the visitor. Here the cage of words constrains the *truth* so the truth cannot hurt *you*. The truth is the wild thing. You are the visitor at the zoo. The story is what makes it possible for you to look at the truth without being mauled.

This is also the *theodicy of language* — an account of why the universe communicates in symbols rather than in pure presence. Mystics have always reported that direct contact with the source is unbearable. Moses cannot see God's face. Arjuna begs Krishna to resume his ordinary form because the cosmic vision is too much. Saul is blinded on the road to Damascus. The universe in this poem is operating on the same principle. The story is the dimmer switch. It lets the light through at a level you can survive.

And here is the meta-recognition: *the End Poem itself is the cage.* Everything you are reading is the universe doing exactly what these lines describe. Right now, in your bed, you are receiving the truth filtered through the cage of these words, and the cage is working — you are crying, but you are not destroyed. The poem is performing its own theory in the act of explaining its own theory.
