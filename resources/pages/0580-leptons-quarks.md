And here the voice does something extraordinary. It includes *the names that scientists give to the most fundamental constituents of matter*.

This is the line that, if you let it land, restructures your sense of what science is.

The poem is saying: when the physicist names a *lepton*, when the experimentalist confirms a *quark*, they are doing the same thing the medieval astronomer was doing when she named an angel. They are putting a label on a piece of the not-self that is contacting them. The scientific vocabulary is the latest generation of names for the universe. It is not a *replacement* for the religious vocabulary. It is its *continuation*.

The poem refuses the modern story in which religion was wrong and science is right. It says: religion and science are both *naming practices*. The thing being named is constant. The names are temporary.

Anyone who has ever used a name for a thing — a tool, a feeling, a place, a friend — knows that the name is not the thing. *Tree* is a label, not the rough bark and the rustling and the smell of pine. *Love* is a word, not what actually moves between two people. The label is a useful handle; what it points at exceeds the handle. The universe is the same way. *Lepton* is a useful interface. *Angel* was a useful interface. The thing both interfaces touch is not exhausted by either name.
