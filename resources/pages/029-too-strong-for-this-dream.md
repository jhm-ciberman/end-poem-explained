The other voice answers. Quietly. With finality.

This is the central ethical claim of the poem about why the universe stays its hand. *To tell them how to live is to prevent them living.* If you were given the answer, you would no longer be living a life — you would be executing instructions. The whole point of the dream is that you have to figure it out from inside it, with incomplete information, making real choices that could go wrong. A life that has been spoiler-warned is no longer a life.

This is one of the oldest answers to the problem of why a loving universe permits suffering and confusion. C.S. Lewis put it this way: God could have made us automata that always chose the good, but the value of love and choice depends on the possibility of refusing them. The Sufi poets put it differently — that the veil is itself a gift, because the seeker is constituted by the seeking. The Zen tradition puts it more sharply: the moment you grasp it, you have lost it.

What the poem adds, that I find moving, is the *reluctance*. The universe does not enjoy the silence. It is not aloof. It is choosing the silence the way a parent chooses not to do the homework for the child even though it would be faster — because the doing is the point, and the parent loves the child enough to refuse to short-circuit the becoming.
