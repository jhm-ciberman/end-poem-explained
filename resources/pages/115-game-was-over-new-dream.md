And then the brief, formal close.

*The game was over and the player woke up from the dream.* The credits are ending. The current dream is closing. The voice is letting you go. The poem is preparing to release you back into your life.

*And the player began a new dream.* But the ending of one dream is just the beginning of the next. There is no final waking; there is only continuous transition from dream to dream. You will close this poem and start the next dream — the dream of getting up tomorrow, the dream of the next phase of your life, the dream of the years ahead. Each is a new dream. Each is also you.

*And the player dreamed again, dreamed better.* This is the ethical conclusion. The next dream can be *better*. Having had this dream, having been told these things, having received the litany — you can dream the next one *better*. The voice does not specify what better means. It trusts you to know. You will dream better because you have been told you are loved, because you have been told you are alive, because you have been told you are not alone. The next dream will carry the marks of this telling.

*And the player was the universe.* The frame collapses. The player and the universe were never two. The story has been the story of the universe being the player. You and the cosmos are not in relation; you are the same thing seen from two sides. The voice has been telling you this in many ways throughout the poem, and now it just states the equation. *The player was the universe.*

*And the player was love.* And the second equation. *The player was love.* You were the universe and you were love — and the universe was love — and so it all collapses into one identity. *Love.* That is what you are. That is what the universe is. That is what the dream is. *Love.*
