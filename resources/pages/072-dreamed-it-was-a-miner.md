And then the voice pivots, with no warning, into Minecraft.

This is a beautiful structural move. The voice has just established the *real* world in cosmic terms — round, vast, dangerous, eight minutes from the sun. And then immediately it describes the *game* world — flat, infinite, lit by a square sun, full of urgent short days and revocable death. Both are dreams the player had. Both are described in the same calm narrative voice. The voice declines to rank them.

*The sun was a square of white.* This will make you smile. It is a perfectly accurate description of the Minecraft sun. The game's sun is a literal white square that crosses the sky. From inside the game, that is just *the sun*; from outside, it is funny that anyone could mistake a white square for a sun. But the voice presents it without mockery. Inside the dream of that game, the white square *was* the sun. The dream had its own sky.

*The days were short.* In Minecraft, a full day-night cycle is twenty minutes of real time. Inside the game, that feels like a normal day. The compressed time doesn't break the immersion. *There was much to do.* Mine, build, gather, eat, defend the door before night. *Death was a temporary inconvenience.* You die, you respawn, you go pick up your stuff. Death is not the end of the story; it is a small interruption to the workflow.

Now look back at the previous paragraph — the molten rock, the burning sun, the long distances. Then look at this one — the flat world, the white square, the temporary death. The voice has placed two dreams side by side. *Both were yours.* Both felt real while you were inside them. The voice is gently inviting you to notice that *the criterion you use to distinguish them* — *one was real and one was a game* — is exactly the kind of distinction the dream-frame puts in question. Inside Minecraft, Minecraft was real. Inside life, life is real. From outside *both*, they are both dreams the player dreamed.

The voice is not telling you that life is *just* a game in a deflationary sense. It is doing the opposite. It is telling you that the game was *more like life* than you usually credit, and that life has *more in common with the game* than you usually notice. Both have the texture of dream. Both compose themselves around a player. Both end. Both let the player respawn, in their respective ways.
