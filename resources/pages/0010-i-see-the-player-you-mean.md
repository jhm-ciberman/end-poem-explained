We arrive in the middle of a conversation. That's the first thing. We don't get an opening, a frame, a "once upon a time" — we drop in late, as if these two voices have been talking about you for a while before you walked in. There's something both unsettling and tender about being the subject of a conversation that started without you. Someone has been pointing you out.

Two voices, never named. The structure echoes ancient theological dialogues — the angels in *Faust*, the bet between God and Satan in the Book of Job (where Job is discussed before he is tested), the chorus that comments on the hero in Greek tragedy. These two are observers, watchers, perhaps facets of one larger consciousness. They will eventually reveal themselves as "the universe." But here, at the start, the ambiguity is doing intentional work. You don't get to know who is watching. You only know that you are watched, and that the watching is not hostile.
