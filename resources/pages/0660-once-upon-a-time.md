The oldest opening in any human language. *Once upon a time.* Every culture has its variant. *Había una vez. Es war einmal. Mukashi mukashi.* The phrase signals: *what follows is a story, you are safe to listen, drop your defenses, the truth is going to come at you sideways.*

But notice the cleverness. Most fairy tales begin *once upon a time, there was a princess* or *a king* or *a poor woodcutter*. This one begins *there was a player*. The hero of the tale is the one who is *playing*. The vocation of the protagonist is to be inside the game. From the very first sentence, the poem has placed the act of playing at the center of what it means to exist.

And there is a small temporal gentleness in *once upon a time*. The phrase places the story in the past tense, the *long ago and far away*. It frames the player as a finished thing, a completed life that can be looked at from outside. This is going to matter. The voice is about to tell you your own story as if it were already over — as if you had already lived it all and the telling were a remembrance. The frame lets the universe describe your life with a kind of panoramic compassion that you cannot have for yourself while you are still inside it.
