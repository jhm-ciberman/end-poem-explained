Another dream. Another mode of existence the player sometimes inhabited. *Lost in a story.* The reader's experience. The watcher's experience. The hour or the evening or the weekend you spent so deep in a novel or a film or a series that the world around you went thin, and the world inside the story went thick, and you were *there* — among the characters, in the city of the book, walking the streets of the imagined place.

This too is a dream the player has. The voice catalogs it as one more. The novel is a small private world your mind enters. The film is a small private world your eyes enter. While you are inside, that world is real to you. The voice is again refusing to rank dreams. *Lost in a story* is not a metaphor for being inattentive. It is a real mode of existence that humans regularly enter.

If you have ever made anything that pulls another person into a small world — a story, a song, a meal that turned a kitchen into a gathering, a game you dreamed up for kids on a rainy afternoon, a room you arranged so a friend would feel held in it — the voice's casual inclusion of this mode might land with extra weight. The kinds of small worlds humans make for each other are also dreams the universe is naming as a real form of being.
