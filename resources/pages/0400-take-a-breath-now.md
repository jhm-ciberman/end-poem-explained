And here the poem reaches across the screen and touches you.

Up to this point, everything could be read as text *about* you. Now it is text *to* you, and what it asks of you is the most ancient spiritual instruction in every contemplative tradition on the planet: *breathe*.

*Take a breath, now.* Present tense. Imperative. The universe is asking you to do something physical, in your body, while you read. And the asking is not metaphorical. If you are reading this in bed, the poem wants you to literally inhale. To notice the air entering you. To feel the small expansion of the chest.

This is the *first* technique of every meditative tradition. Pranayama in yoga. Anapanasati in Buddhism — mindfulness of breathing. Hesychasm in Eastern Orthodoxy synchronizes the Jesus Prayer with the breath. Sufis use breath as the vehicle of dhikr. The breath is the bridge between the autonomic and the chosen, between body and mind, between the universe outside and the universe inside. To return to the breath is to return to the only place where being-alive is actually happening.

And note what just happened structurally. The poem has been talking about how the truth is too strong, how the dream is necessary, how the voices must hold back. And then, at the moment of contact, the universe's instruction is not *understand this* or *believe this* or *figure out this*. It is *breathe*. The breath is the truth that can be safely transmitted. Everything the poem cannot say, the breath knows already.
