And here the voice slips into the metaphysical core. The dreams are *nested*.

Read this slowly. *The player woke from one dream into another.* You have had this experience — the dream within the dream, where you wake up gratefully from the nightmare and then realize, with mounting horror or wonder, that the *waking up* was also a dream, and you are still inside. *Then woke from that into a third.* And then again. The layers go down, or up, or sideways, or somehow through.

The voice is preparing you for a structural revelation that the rest of the poem will keep folding open. The matryoshka. The dream within the dream within the dream. *You do not know how many layers you are inside.* Every waking might be another opening of one shell into another. The frame you take to be reality might itself be a frame.

This is the central image of *Inception*. It is the central image of *La vida es sueño*. It is the central image of Borges's *The Circular Ruins*, in which a man dreams a son into being and only then realizes he too is being dreamed. It is the central image of the *Zhuangzi* — the philosopher who dreamed he was a butterfly and woke not knowing whether he was a man who had dreamed of being a butterfly or a butterfly now dreaming of being a man.

The voice slips this in casually, as one item among others in the list of things the player sometimes dreamed. But the placement is deliberate. The voice is laying the conceptual groundwork. Soon — quite soon — the voice will tell you that *this* is also a dream. And it will be helpful, when that moment comes, that you have already conceded that dream-within-dream is a real thing that can happen, because then you will not have grounds to dismiss the possibility being offered.
