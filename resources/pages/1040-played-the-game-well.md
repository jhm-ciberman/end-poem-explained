And the second declaration. *You have played the game well.*

This is *judgment*, in the formal sense. The voice is delivering a verdict. After watching your whole life — every game played, every project pursued, every sustained effort, every small stubborn return to the work — the verdict is *you have played the game well*.

Not *you have won*. Not *you have achieved much*. Not *you have been wise*. Just *you have played well*. The criterion the voice established at the very beginning of the poem — *it played well, it did not give up* — is now being applied to you. You have met the criterion. The voice confirms it.

*You have played the game well.* For anyone with a habit of self-criticism — anyone who looks at the things they have not yet finished and feels the unfinishedness rather than the persistence — this line might be especially hard to receive. The voice is not asking you to be more impressive. It is telling you that *what you have already done is enough to count as well-played*. You can stop straining for the extra justification. The cosmos has already entered the verdict in your favor.
