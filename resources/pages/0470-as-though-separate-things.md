And then this, which is the philosophical hinge that the breath was preparing you for.

Your body, the voice says, is *touching the universe at every point*. This is literally true. The skin is the boundary, but it is a boundary that is constantly in contact — air on every square millimeter, photons landing on the eye, sound pressure moving the eardrum, gravity pulling on every atom, the chair pressing back on the body that presses on it. There is no point where you are not touching the universe. The touching is total, continuous, uninterrupted.

And the touching gives you the *illusion* of separateness. Because you can feel the boundary, you assume there *is* a boundary. The skin defines a self. Inside the skin: me. Outside the skin: not-me. This is the basic illusion of the dream, and the poem has now named it.

*As though you were separate things.* You appear to be a separate thing. You feel like a separate thing. You experience yourself as bounded, finite, a self walking around in a world.

*As though we were separate things.* And the universe — the cosmic thing the voices represent — appears to be a separate thing from you. As though it were over there and you were over here and the conversation was happening across a gap.

But the *as though* is doing all the work. *As though.* Meaning: it appears so, but it is not so. The separateness is the appearance. The unity is the reality. You and the universe are not two things touching at a boundary; you are one thing experiencing itself as two, by virtue of having a boundary that lets the touching feel like meeting.

This is the central insight of the *Upanishads*. *Tat tvam asi* — that thou art. The thing you take to be the universe and the thing you take to be yourself are the same thing, briefly arranged so as to seem like two. It is the central insight of Mahayana Buddhism — the boundary between self and world is conventionally useful and ultimately false. It is the central insight of the Christian mystics — the soul and God are not, in the deepest analysis, separable. It is what Alan Watts spent his life saying. It is what Spinoza was trying to articulate. It is what physics keeps half-discovering when it tries to explain why the observer cannot be removed from the observation.

The poem has arrived at this insight not by argument but by *breath*. By having you feel the air in your lungs, the universe has led you to the moment where you can be told: the air is not separate from you, the lungs are not separate from the air, the breathing is not separate from the breather, and the seeming of separateness is the kind illusion that lets you have a life at all.
